Nutritional Gaps and Dietary Choices: An Insight into American Food Habits

22/01/2024

Approximately half of all U.S. adults prioritize healthy eating. Despite this, the standard American diet is deficient in many essential nutrients, as identified by the Dietary Guidelines for Americans. This comprehensive guide, provided by the federal government, aims to meet nutrient needs, promote health, and prevent disease. It has recognized four nutrients that are so scarce in American diets that they’re classified as a 'public health concern.' These crucial nutrients can be found in a range of foods including fruits, vegetables, legumes, dairy products, and certain types of meat.

For example, potassium, one of the nutrients often missing in American diets, is abundant in fruits such as dried raisins, prunes, and apricots. It can also be found in vegetables like potatoes, tomatoes, and broccoli, as well as in lentils, kidney beans, and animal products including milk, yogurt, chicken, and salmon. Calcium is another nutrient that is frequently lacking in American diets. It is present in dairy products like milk, yogurt, and cheese, canned salmon and sardines, and dark leafy greens like spinach and kale.

Despite these nutritional deficiencies, many Americans find solace in the warmth and comfort of fried food during the cold winter months. At the same time, home cooking is gaining popularity as it allows individuals to monitor and control the intake of salt, sugar, and other ingredients. Moreover, there is a rising trend towards vegetarian and vegan lifestyles, leading to questions about the effectiveness of plant-based proteins in supporting muscle maintenance and growth.

Along with these dietary trends, innovations in food technology are shaping the future of food consumption. For instance, the development of cultivated seafood offers a sustainable alternative to traditional fishing methods. This development aligns with the global shift towards alternative proteins and sustainable food practices.

There is also a growing awareness about the nutritional benefits of traditional crops. In many developing countries, there is a move away from the reliance on staple grains like maize towards a diversity of traditional crops such as cowpeas, cassava, and different millets. This shift, viewed as 'revolutionary' by some, could play a significant role in addressing food insecurity and mitigating the effects of climate change on agriculture.
