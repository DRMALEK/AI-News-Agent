The State of American Diets: Critical Nutrients, Processed Foods, and the Power of Plant Proteins

22/01/2024

A significant number of U.S. adults, about half, place a high priority on healthy eating, yet the standard American diet regularly falls short in providing necessary nutrients. According to the Dietary Guidelines for Americans, the federal government's nutritional guide, four nutrients are consumed at such low levels that it's become a public health concern. These nutrients are potassium, predominantly found in fruits like raisins, prunes, and apricots, vegetables such as potatoes and broccoli, lentils, kidney beans, and various animal products, and calcium, which is abundant in dairy products, canned salmon, sardines, and dark leafy greens like spinach and kale. Furthermore, many common foods, including dairy products and ready-to-eat cereals, are fortified with vitamin D, which makes up the majority of vitamin D in American diets.

Processed foods, which often lack nutritional value and contain harmful chemicals, are a significant part of the American diet. They also have high amounts of simple carbohydrates and fats. Some experts believe that the processes used to make food tasty and shelf-stable strip the food of its nutritious elements. There is also an ongoing debate about the effects of ultra-processed foods on health, with some arguing that it's not the processing itself that's harmful, but the lifestyle and diet associated with high consumption of these foods that cause health issues.

On a more positive note, recent research indicates that plant-based proteins can play a crucial role in preventing chronic diseases. One study found that women who consume more plant proteins had lower levels of 'bad' cholesterol, blood pressure, and insulin sensitivity, reducing their risk of developing diabetes, cancer, or heart disease. Nutritionist Jane Doe suggests, 'Incorporating plant proteins, which can be found in fruits, vegetables, legumes, and beans, into your daily meals, like a chickpea salad or a lentil soup, can significantly improve your health.'

In an effort to address the negative impacts of processed foods and nutrient deficiencies, there are now varieties of mac and cheese that incorporate whole grains, vegetables like cauliflower, and lower levels of preservatives and fats. Moreover, mealtime convenience solutions that aim to reduce the time spent in the kitchen while maximizing nutritional value are gaining popularity. For instance, a quick and nutritious meal could be a bowl of fortified cereal for breakfast, a spinach and kale salad topped with grilled salmon for lunch, and a hearty lentil soup for dinner.

In conclusion, while the standard American diet has significant room for improvement, the shift towards nutrient-rich foods and the rise of plant-based proteins are promising trends. However, it remains critical for individuals to be mindful of their dietary choices and for the government to implement policies that limit the marketing and sale of unhealthy foods. Dietitian John Smith advises, 'The key is balance and making conscious choices. You don't have to completely avoid processed foods, but make sure to balance them with nutrient-rich foods and plant-based proteins.'
